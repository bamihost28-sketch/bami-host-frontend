**Palette**: Limited spot-color system. Use two to four ink-like colors on warm paper: fluorescent pink (`#FF4FA3`), teal (`#00A6A6`), sunflower (`#F7C948`), ultramarine (`#3155D4`), tomato (`#F05A3B`), soy black (`#1E1A18`), and cream stock (`#F3E6C8`). Color overlaps should create intentional third tones through multiply blending rather than generic full-spectrum gradients.

**Type**: Editorial print mix: bold poster serif or chunky grotesk for headlines, sober sans or serif for reading text, occasional hand-set caption labels. Headlines can be oversized, tightly composed, and slightly offset; body text remains clean enough to survive grain and color drift. Type should look printed in passes, not rendered as slick digital UI chrome.

**Texture**: Medium-specific print evidence. Halftone dots, paper tooth, overprint, soy-ink grain, smudge, and slight registration drift are central. Implement with SVG noise, halftone masks, `mix-blend-mode: multiply`, low-opacity paper overlays, and 1–3px offset duplicate layers. Texture must be visible but not so strong that utility copy becomes illegible.

**Shape**: Paper and poster geometry. Rectangular sheets, pinned notices, tape-like labels, folded corners, image windows, blocky silhouettes, and cut-paper captions dominate. Border radius is low (`0–6px`) unless imitating soft sticker/tape edges. Shapes should feel assembled from printed matter rather than generated by a component library.

**Motion**: Mostly static, with print-process microinteractions. Use subtle registration-slip hover (`80–160ms`), layered ink-pass reveals (`250–450ms`), poster-pull transitions, or sheet-stack shifts. Avoid elastic toy motion and high-frequency animation; risograph's source medium is tactile and static.

**Spatial**: Editorial spread and poster-grid logic. Use generous margins, asymmetric image blocks, page-like modules, margin notes, and stacked poster cards. Density can be high, but hierarchy should read like a designed zine spread or print-fair program rather than a SaaS dashboard.

**Cultural markers**: fluorescent spot inks; halftone dot fields; visible overprint overlaps; registration drift; cream paper stock; zines and small-run art publishing; indie print-fair schedules; duplicator drum/process references.

**Non-negotiables**: limited spot-color palette + visible halftone/grain texture + overprint or registration-drift cues + editorial poster composition. Without medium-specific print behavior, this collapses into generic colorful retro illustration.

## Connotation

**Nostalgic quotation / contemporary revival** — contemporary design communities use risograph both as affection for small-run print culture and as a living craft process. The right tone is intelligent, tactile, and handmade, not fake distressed sloppiness. It can be playful, but it should not become generic grunge or punk abrasion.

## Scope

**Suitable for**: arts organizations, zine shops, indie publishers, event programs, editorial features, portfolio sites, and community/cultural calendars. **NOT suitable for**: mission-critical dashboards, dense financial tables, luxury minimal commerce, or contexts where print texture will be read as low fidelity.

**Related / Subsets**: Close to *punk-zine* through indie publishing, but punk is photocopy rebellion and ransom-note collage while risograph is spot-ink craft. Close to *desktop-publishing* through page logic, but not early-computer bitmap abundance. Close to *grunge-typography* through texture, but risograph texture comes from ink and registration, not digital distress.
