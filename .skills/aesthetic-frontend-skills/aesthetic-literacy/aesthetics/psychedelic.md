**Palette**: Clashing, vibrating, full-spectrum color designed to create retinal vibration: hot magenta `#FF00FF`, electric cyan `#00FFFF`, acid yellow `#FFFF00`, deep purple `#4B0082`, lime green `#32CD32`, orange `#FF6600`. High saturation, high contrast, deliberately uncomfortable pairings. Victor Moscoso's "dayglow" palette — inspired by Josef Albers' color theory but weaponized for sensory overload. CSS: `filter: contrast(1.5) saturate(2)` for the full effect.

**Type**: Illegible flowing hand-lettering — Wes Wilson's sculptural font where letterforms melt into each other. Letters warp, stretch, and curve to fill available space. Google Fonts: Nothing standard — use display faces (Psychedelic Revival fonts from Putracetol Studio, Bellbottom, or custom `@font-face`). CSS: `transform: skew(), rotate(), scale()` to warp type; `letter-spacing` varied per character. Type as visual texture, not reading — illegibility is the point.

**Texture**: Kaleidoscopic symmetry (CSS: `conic-gradient`, radial repeat patterns). Paisley and fractal fills. Liquid/warped distortions. CSS: `filter: blur() + contrast()` for gooey melt effects, `background: conic-gradient()` for mandala energy. Horror vacui — every surface filled with pattern, nothing empty.

**Shape**: Organic curves, Art Nouveau whiplash lines, no straight edges. Border-radius maximalism — `border-radius: 50%` everywhere. Fluid blob shapes, paisley teardrops, undulating waves. Geometry is forbidden — the organic rebellion against the grid.

**Motion**: Melting, flowing, kaleidoscopic rotation. CSS: slow continuous rotation on mandala elements, `@keyframes` for liquid morphing, parallax-like depth shifts. Motion should feel immersive and slightly disorienting — the visual equivalent of a trip.

**Spatial**: Dense and symmetrical — horror vacui (fear of empty space). Every surface filled. Radial symmetry dominates: `display: flex; justify-content: center;` with elements radiating from center. Poster-proportioned. The opposite of white space philosophy.

**Cultural markers**: Fillmore concert posters (Bill Graham Presents), album covers (Jimi Hendrix, Grateful Dead, Pink Floyd), Oz magazine, underground comix, light shows. Born in San Francisco's Haight-Ashbury, 1966. Counterculture visual language — design as expanded consciousness.

**Non-negotiables**: clashing vibrating palette + illegible flowing type + organic curve geometry + kaleidoscopic symmetry + horror vacui density

**Vs. Pop Art**: Near opposites. Pop: flat, clean, commercial, hard-edged, ironic, readable. Psychedelic: organic, immersive, illegible, fluid, ecstatic. They share 1960s timing and a reaction against Abstract Expressionism — nothing else. Two independent entries are essential.

## Google Fonts

- **Monoton** — single-weight geometric display face with art-deco-meets-psychedelic curves; evokes 1960s poster lettering energy
- **Fugaz One** — bold, rounded display face with organic flow; captures the sculptural, filled-space quality of Wes Wilson lettering
- **Lobster** — flowing script with exaggerated curves and swashes; approximates the fluid, hand-drawn feel of Fillmore poster type
- **Bungee Shade** — layered, dimensional display face with drop-shadow built in; useful for the 3D/layered lettering effect common in revival psychedelia

> [!tip] Note
> Authentic psychedelic type — Wes Wilson's sculptural font, Rick Griffin's hand-lettering, Victor Moscoso's custom faces — has no direct Google Fonts equivalent. The recommendations above are the closest approximations in spirit. For maximum authenticity, use `@font-face` with Putracetol Studio's psychedelic revival fonts or custom hand-lettered SVG.

## Connotation

**Authentic immersion.** Psychedelic aesthetics are earnest and ecstatic — they seek to induce expanded consciousness, not comment on it. The visual language is designed to feel immersive, not ironic: clashing colors, illegible type, and kaleidoscopic symmetry are deployed sincerely to create a sensory experience that mirrors altered states. Unlike Pop Art's ironic distance or nostalgic quotation's self-consciousness, Psychedelic aims for genuine perceptual transformation. In contemporary use, this earnestness reads as countercultural authenticity — a rejection of irony in favor of full-spectrum sensory engagement.

## Scope

**Immersive, experiential, and entertainment contexts.** Psychedelic aesthetics excel at music festival websites, album art and artist promotion, immersive brand experiences, creative agency portfolios, and experiential marketing. The organic curves, kaleidoscopic symmetry, and horror vacui density translate well to hero sections and full-page visual experiences.

**Avoid for:** readability-critical interfaces (illegible type is a feature, not a bug, but it destroys information architecture), corporate/professional contexts, data visualization (the palette is deliberately clashing and vibrating — it competes with data), accessibility-sensitive applications (vestibular triggers from motion effects, cognitive overload from density), and any context where clarity and calm are required. Psychedelic is a full-commitment aesthetic — half-measures read as weak pastiche rather than authentic reference.

## Subsets / Related

- *Fillmore Poster Style* — the canonical San Francisco concert-poster variant (Wes Wilson, Rick Griffin, Victor Moscoso, Stanley Mouse); the most visually distinct and historically documented form. Defined by sculptural illegible lettering, clashing dayglow palettes, and Art Nouveau revival curves.
- *British Psychedelia (Oz Magazine, Hapshash)* — the London variant with more collage elements, Victorian-Edwardian imagery, and a darker palette. Less flowing-organic, more juxtaposition-driven.
- *Psychedelic Light Show* — the liquid-projection variant (oil-and-water light shows, lava-lamp fluid dynamics); translates to CSS via `filter: blur() + contrast()` gooey effects and slow-morphing blob animations.
- Distinct from **[[pop-art]]** — near opposites sharing only 1960s timing. Pop: flat, clean, commercial, hard-edged, ironic. Psychedelic: organic, immersive, illegible, fluid, ecstatic.
- Distinct from **[[rave-flyer]]** — Psychedelic is organic, hand-drawn, warm-toned (orange/yellow/magenta), with flowing Art Nouveau-influenced lettering. Rave is synthetic, digital, cold-neon (cyan/magenta/lime/ultraviolet), with machine-precision geometric type and 3D extrusion. Psychedelic comes from expanded consciousness (1966–1972); Rave comes from the club floor (1991–1997).
