# Examples — Aesthetic Literacy

## Example prompts

Tasks that should activate this skill:

1. "I want my app to have a Y2K vibe. What does that actually mean in design terms?"
2. "Give our product a 60s psychedelic aesthetic."
3. "What's the difference between vaporwave and synthwave?"
4. "We want something retro but not old-fashioned. Kind of futuristic retro."
5. "Describe dark academia as a design aesthetic. What are its defining visual properties?"

---

## Ideal response patterns

**For prompt 1 (Y2K — explain the aesthetic):**

The agent names the aesthetic clearly (Y2K, 1998–2003), characterizes it across all 7 dimensions, and identifies the non-negotiables:

> Y2K as a design aesthetic draws from late-90s/early-00s consumer technology optimism — the cultural moment when the internet and digital hardware felt genuinely futuristic and magical.
>
> **Palette**: Chrome, silver, iridescent holographic gradients, acid green/cyan/hot pink/magenta accents, often on white or silver backgrounds.
> **Type**: Rounded bubbly sans-serifs (Eurostile, custom inflated letterforms), mixed-case, heavy display outlines.
> **Texture**: Metallic sheen, plastic gloss, translucent jelly-like surfaces, lenticular effects.
> **Shape**: Inflated bubbly geometry — pill shapes, rounded rectangles taken to extremes, beveled 3D orbs.
> **Motion**: Springy and elastic — elements pop in with bounce; cursor trails; interface chrome animates.
> **Spatial**: Dense, layered, multiple panels; high visual complexity.
> **Cultural markers**: CRT monitors, loading bars, starfield backgrounds, "under construction" GIFs, holographic sticker language.
>
> Non-negotiables: metallic/chrome surface + bubbly geometry + acid/iridescent color. Remove any one of these and the aesthetic becomes "vaguely retro digital" rather than distinctly Y2K.
>
> Connotation today (2026): nostalgia + camp + Gen-Z reclamation. The aesthetic carries ironic warmth, not earnest futurism.

**For prompt 5 (dark academia — full characterization):**

> Dark academia is a contemporary aesthetic (emerged on Tumblr ~2015, peaked via TikTok ~2020) that romanticizes the visual culture of elite 19th-century European scholarship.
>
> **Palette**: Sepia, warm brown, charcoal, forest green, deep burgundy, aged cream — all desaturated, as if lit by candlelight or filtered through old photographic paper.
> **Type**: Old-style serifs (Garamond, Playfair Display, EB Garamond) — classical editorial with high-contrast thick/thin strokes.
> **Texture**: Aged paper, book cloth, leather, wood paneling — analog surfaces with patina.
> **Shape**: Classical architectural motifs (arches, columns), bookshelf grids, coat-of-arms forms; symmetrical and institutional.
> **Motion**: Slow, deliberate — page turns, slow fades; no kinetic energy.
> **Spatial**: Dense and scholarly — text-heavy, multiple column layouts, no excessive whitespace; library density.
> **Cultural markers**: Open books, quill/fountain pen, candles, ivy, telescopes, globes, classical sculpture.
>
> Connotation: intellectual aspiration + melancholy beauty + the romanticism of learning. Carries class/institutional undertones (Oxbridge, Hogwarts-adjacent) that should be considered when applying to products with diverse or international audiences.

## Anti-examples

**Anti-example 1: Aesthetic reduced to palette**

> "Y2K means bright colors — use chrome silver, electric blue, and hot pink."

Why it fails: This identifies one dimension (palette) and stops. A design with Y2K palette but neutral sans-serif type, flat surfaces, and grid-clean spatial layout will not read as Y2K — it will read as "neon-colored flat design." All 7 dimensions must be addressed, and the non-negotiables (bubbly geometry, metallic surface) are as important as palette.

**Anti-example 2: Conflating neighboring aesthetics**

> "Vaporwave and synthwave are basically the same thing — both are retro-80s neon aesthetics."

Why it fails: These aesthetics share palette adjacency but are distinct in origin, cultural loading, and formal properties. Vaporwave uses pastel-faded palettes, VHS artifacts, Greek sculpture, and hollow surreal spaces — its connotation is ironic nostalgia for consumer capitalism. Synthwave uses neon-on-dark, retro-futurist 80s visual language, and grid-perspective voids — its connotation is earnest 80s sci-fi romance. Conflating them produces a hybrid with neither aesthetic coherence nor cultural clarity.

**Anti-example 3: Historical aesthetic without connotation audit**

> "Use Art Deco for our new fintech app — it looks premium and sophisticated."

Why it fails: Art Deco is formally premium, but its connotation includes 1920s Gilded Age excess and pre-Depression luxury. For a contemporary fintech product, applying Art Deco without connotation awareness can read as either "timeless luxury" OR "ostentatious 1920s revivalism" depending on execution. The agent should surface this ambiguity before confirming the direction.

**Anti-example 4: Disambiguation skipped**

> User: "Make it feel kind of retro." Agent: "Here's your retro color palette: warm oranges, browns, and avocado green..."

Why it fails: "Retro" spans at least 5 distinct aesthetics (70s Americana, 80s pop, Y2K, early internet, skeuomorphic hardware). The agent must ask one targeted disambiguation question before producing any design decisions.
