**Palette**: Cream (#f5f0e8), chrome silver (#c0c0c0), cherry red (#cc3333), turquoise (#40b8b0), mint green (#a8d8b9), dark charcoal (#2c2c2c); neutral base with metallic accent and strategic color pops — optimistic but restrained

**Type**: Geometric sans-serif — Futura, Kabel, Neutraface; low x-height, circular 'O', pointed A/M/N vertices; type is sleek and aerodynamic, often set in horizontal bands or integrated into architectural lettering

**Texture**: Smooth/glossy surfaces — polished chrome, lacquered wood, glass block, vitrolite panels; the surface must look fast; metallic gradients suggest speed; no rough or handmade texture

**Shape**: Horizontal speed lines, teardrop curves, fully rounded corners, porthole circles; the rectangle softened into an aerodynamic body; no sharp angles, no jagged edges — everything designed to reduce drag

**Motion**: Sweeping horizontal movement — speed lines, cantilevered forms, and the illusion of forward thrust; motion is implied in static forms through elongation and streamlining; smooth, unhurried gliding

**Spatial**: Strong horizontal orientation — wide layouts, thin horizontal rule lines, low-profile headers; cantilevered elements extend outward; generous negative space around streamlined central forms; the "ocean liner" silhouette — long and low

**Cultural markers**: Chrome diner architecture; the 1934 Chrysler Airflow; Greyhound bus terminals; Miami Beach Art Deco district (Streamline variant); the *Normandie* ocean liner interiors; the "world of tomorrow" World's Fair optimism

**Non-negotiables**: horizontal speed-line orientation + fully rounded corners + chrome/cream palette

## Connotation
**Mode: nostalgic quotation.** Streamline Moderne is firmly locked to its era (1930–1945). Every application today carries a quotation mark — it says "remember the world of tomorrow?" It cannot be deployed neutrally; it always evokes chrome diners, ocean liners, and Depression-era techno-optimism. The aesthetic is almost never used authentically (the world has moved on) nor ironically (its optimism resists cynicism). It survives as fond nostalgia for a future that never quite arrived.

## Scope
**Suitable:** Retro-themed restaurant and hospitality sites, vintage product launches or limited-edition brand campaigns, transportation and automotive history projects, event sites for mid-century themed gatherings, portfolio pieces demonstrating period-design fluency, museum exhibition micro-sites. The horizontal-speed-line language is also useful as a secondary accent pattern on otherwise contemporary pages.

**NOT suitable:** Cutting-edge SaaS or tech platforms, financial dashboards, medical/healthcare interfaces, corporate intranets, e-commerce for contemporary products, anything requiring trust in "modern" technology. Streamline Moderne reads as charmingly dated — deploying it for a fintech app would undermine credibility. Use it where period charm IS the value proposition.

## Subsets / Related
- [[art-deco]] — parent/adjacent style; Streamline Moderne evolved from Art Deco's vertical, ornamental geometry into horizontal, minimal aerodynamic forms; can be thought of as "Art Deco stripped of ornament and turned sideways"
- [[bauhaus]] — philosophical overlap; both embrace industrial materials and functional honesty, but Bauhaus is orthogonal/grid-based while Streamline is curved/aerodynamic; Bauhaus is European/intellectual; Streamline is American/populist
